What is VO2 Max?

VO2 Max measures the maximum amount of oxygen your body can utilize during intense exercise, expressed in milliliters per kilogram per minute (mL/kg/min). It's influenced by genetics, age, training, and factors like altitude. Higher values indicate better aerobic capacity, correlating with endurance in sports like running or cycling. Formulas from sources like the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) include:

  • Resting Heart Rate Method: VO2 Max ≈ 15 × (HRmax / HRrest), where HRmax is 220 − age.
  • Rockport Walk Test: VO2 Max = 132.853 − (0.0769 × weight in lbs) − (0.3877 × age) + (6.315 × gender; 1 for male, 0 for female) − (3.2649 × time in minutes) − (0.1565 × heart rate).
  • Cooper Test: VO2 Max = (distance in meters − 504.9) / 44.73 for a 12-minute run.

Common Questions About VO2 Max

How accurate are these estimates?

Field tests like Rockport are within 10–15% of lab results—great for home use, but pros may opt for gas analysis.

Can VO2 Max improve?

Yes, with consistent cardio and strength training—recalculate every 4–6 weeks.

What's a good VO2 Max?

Varies by age/gender: 35–40 mL/kg/min is average for adults; over 50 is excellent.

Does it differ for sports?

Runners often use Cooper; cyclists may prefer FTP-based methods—adapt as needed.
