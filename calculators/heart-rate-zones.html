What are Heart Rate Zones?

Heart rate zones divide your effort levels into categories (typically 1–5) based on a percentage of your maximum heart rate (MHR). This guides intensity: Zone 1 for easy recovery, up to Zone 5 for all-out sprints. Accurate zones prevent burnout and maximize benefits like improved cardio fitness or fat loss.

Our calculator supports multiple methods:

  • Standard (220 − age): A simple, widely used estimate for MHR.
  • Tanaka (208 − 0.7 × age): More accurate for older adults, based on research.
  • Karvonen: Uses resting heart rate (RHR) for precise, individualized zones—ideal for athletes.

Backed by exercise physiology from sources like the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM), these formulas help tailor zones to your fitness level.

How to Use Our Heart Rate Zone Calculator

  1. Enter Your Age: The foundation for estimating MHR.
  2. Add Resting Heart Rate (Optional): Measure your pulse at rest (e.g., morning) for Karvonen accuracy.
  3. Select Method: Choose Standard, Tanaka, or Karvonen.
  4. Calculate Zones: Get instant breakdowns in beats per minute (bpm) for each zone.

Example: For a 30-year-old with MHR of 190 bpm:

  • Zone 1 (Recovery): 95–114 bpm — Light activity, warm-up.
  • Zone 2 (Fat Burn): 114–133 bpm — Endurance, fat burning.
  • Zone 3 (Aerobic): 133–152 bpm — Cardio fitness.
  • Zone 4 (Anaerobic): 152–171 bpm — Speed and power.
  • Zone 5 (Maximum): 171–190 bpm — Peak effort, bursts.

Why Calculate Your Heart Rate Zones?

  • Targeted Workouts: Train smarter—burn fat in Zone 2 or build speed in Zone 4 for better results.
  • Injury Prevention: Stay in safe zones to avoid strain, especially for beginners or those with health concerns.
  • Progress Tracking: Adjust as fitness improves; lower RHR means evolving zones.
  • Free and Private: Quick calculations with no data storage—use it anywhere on mobile.

Common Questions About Heart Rate Zones

  • How do I measure resting heart rate? Count your pulse for 60 seconds upon waking—aim for consistency.
  • Which method is best? Karvonen for accuracy if you know RHR; Standard for quick estimates.
  • Can zones change? Yes, with age, fitness gains, or weight loss—recalculate regularly.
